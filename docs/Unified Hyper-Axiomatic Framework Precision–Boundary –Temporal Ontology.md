## Precision–Boundary–Temporal Ontology for Autism-like, Psychopathy-like, BPD-like, and Neurotypical Trajectories

This framework treats neurotypes as **dynamic trajectories**, not fixed essences. It is a speculative computational model, not a diagnostic tool. The corrected version avoids saying “autism causes BPD,” “psychopathy is immune to trauma,” or “autism and psychopathy are simple opposites.” Instead, it models all of them as different **state families** inside a multi-domain mind-space.

# I. 12 Patterns / Correlations / Points of Relativity

[

[

Psychopathy-like distance may arise from reduced affective resonance, dominance-seeking, instrumental reward, or low emotional cost.

Same surface coordinate. Different internal engine.

# IV. 48 Novel Cutting-Edge Groundbreaking Discoveries / Hypotheses

## Domain A — Boundary Dynamics

### 1. Boundary strength is orthogonal to emotional content

Two people can have equally high boundary, but one is protecting against overload while the other is optimizing control.

---

### 2. Social distance is not a diagnosis

Autistic shutdown, trauma freeze, strategic concealment, depression, and low affect can all look like “distance.”

---

### 3. Autism and psychopathy mimic each other only at low resolution

At high resolution, they diverge through affective precision, moral cost, sensory load, reward weighting, and threat response.

---

### 4. Sensory porosity can coexist with social rigidity

A person can be socially closed and sensorily flooded at the same time.

---

### 5. Boundary collapse and boundary hardening are sibling failure modes

Overload may produce dependency, meltdown, and emotional flooding, or it may produce shutdown, isolation, and rigidity.

---

### 6. Boundary injury requires repeated invalidation

One painful event may wound. Repeated invalidation teaches the system that its own signals cannot be trusted.

---

### 7. Attachment boundary volatility predicts BPD-like states better than global boundary

The key variable is not total boundary but:

[
\left|
\frac{dB_{attachment}}{dt}
\right|
]

---

### 8. Recovery from boundary collapse is hysteretic

The path out is not the reverse of the path in.

[
F_{recovery}\neq -F_{collapse}
]

---

## Domain B — Precision Dynamics

### 9. Precision is multi-channel

There is no single “high precision mind.” There are sensory, affective, cognitive, instrumental, threat, moral, and reward precisions.

---

### 10. Affective precision is a major autism–psychopathy separator

Autism-like states may involve intense or confusing affective resonance. Psychopathy-like states may involve lower affective cost with preserved strategic modeling.

---

### 11. Cognitive empathy and affective empathy can dissociate

A person may feel deeply but interpret poorly. Another may interpret accurately but feel little.

---

### 12. Alexithymia is a communication bottleneck, not emotional absence

[
I(E_{felt};E_{expressed}) \downarrow
]

Emotion exists, but less information reaches language or expression.

---

### 13. Hyper-empathy can produce avoidance

Too much resonance can cause withdrawal because contact becomes overwhelming.

---

### 14. Rule precision has different moral signatures

Rule-following can come from care, fear, pattern stability, social learning, dominance, or instrumental calculation.

---

### 15. Prediction error weighting separates overload from indifference

Autistic overload is high weighted error. Psychopathy-like indifference may be low affective error weighting.

---

### 16. Masking requires expensive precision control

Good masking requires high social monitoring, high inhibition, and suppression of sensory/emotional reactions.

---

## Domain C — Temporal Dynamics

### 17. Temporal horizon splits by valence

Reward future, punishment future, attachment future, routine future, and novelty future are not the same clock.

---

### 18. Predictability is a primary currency in autism-like systems

For some systems:

[
Reward < Predictability
]

Routine is not stubbornness. It can be temporal regulation.

---

### 19. Routine disruption is temporal injury

A broken plan can damage future simulation, not just convenience.

---

### 20. Psychopathy-like reward horizons may outpace punishment horizons

Reward may remain vivid while future punishment becomes discounted.

---

### 21. Trauma bends time backward

Threat history can make the past more active than the future.

[
T_{past} > T_{future}
]

---

### 22. Addiction is temporal collapse

Immediate reward dominates because future punishment loses force.

---

### 23. Sleep regulates temporal coherence

Low sleep fragments reward, punishment, and social prediction.

---

### 24. Burnout shortens future horizon

When energy collapses, long-term planning becomes biologically expensive.

---

### 26. Clinical instruments are shadows of moving trajectories
